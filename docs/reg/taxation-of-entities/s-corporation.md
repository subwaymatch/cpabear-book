# S Corporation

An **S corporation** is a corporation that makes a valid election to be taxed under **Subchapter S**. For REG, it blends corporate law with pass-through taxation, making it a favorite area for eligibility, basis, and distribution questions.

## Core legal and tax profile

- **Owners:** Shareholders.
- **Liability:** Shareholders generally have limited liability.
- **Federal return:** **Form 1120-S**.
- **Tax character:** Usually a pass-through entity with items reported to shareholders on **Schedule K-1**.

## Eligibility and election

To qualify, the corporation generally must:

- Be a domestic corporation
- Have no more than **100 shareholders**
- Have only eligible shareholders
- Have only **one class of stock**
- Timely file a valid S election

A violation of these rules can terminate the election.

## Taxation of operations

### Pass-through treatment

Most income, deductions, gains, losses, and credits pass through to shareholders. Unlike a C corporation, the S corporation generally does not pay federal income tax at the entity level.

### Basis matters

A shareholder's ability to deduct passed-through losses depends heavily on **stock basis** and **debt basis**.

### Compensation issues

Shareholder-employees who perform services must receive **reasonable compensation** as wages. This is a major exam issue because wages are subject to payroll taxes, while distributions are not.

## Distributions

S corporation distributions are often simpler than C corporation distributions, but basis is still critical.

- Distributions are generally tax free to the extent of stock basis.
- Distributions in excess of stock basis generally create capital gain.
- If the corporation has accumulated E&P from prior C corporation years, additional ordering rules may apply.

The **accumulated adjustments account (AAA)** often appears in exam questions involving distributions from former C corporations.

## Termination issues

An S election can terminate by:

- Voluntary revocation
- Failure to meet eligibility requirements
- Excess passive investment income problems in certain former C corporation situations

## When businesses choose an S corporation

S corporations are often attractive for closely held businesses that want:

- Liability protection
- Pass-through taxation
- Potential payroll tax planning compared with sole proprietorship or partnership treatment

They are less useful when owners need multiple classes of stock or want institutional investors.

## Advantages and disadvantages

| Topic | S Corporation |
| --- | --- |
| Liability protection | Strong |
| Tax regime | Pass-through taxation |
| Ownership restrictions | Significant |
| Capital raising | More limited than a C corporation |
| Exam focus | Eligibility, basis, distributions, and reasonable compensation |

## REG quick hits

- S corporations file Form 1120-S.
- They are usually pass-through entities.
- Eligibility requirements are strict.
- Only one class of stock is allowed.
- Loss deductions depend on shareholder basis.
- Reasonable compensation is a recurring exam issue.

## Bottom line

An S corporation gives small business owners corporate liability protection without regular C corporation taxation, but the election comes with strict eligibility limits. On REG, know the qualification rules, basis limits, distribution ordering, and wage-versus-distribution issues.
